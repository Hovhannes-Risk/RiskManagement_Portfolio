# Methodology — Risk Management Portfolio

*Technical methodology behind the analytical work in this portfolio.
Covers data cleaning, the odds-field discovery, detection logic for
bots and multi-accounts, risk scoring, and reporting.*

---

## 1. Dataset — fully synthetic, deterministically reproducible

- **526,232** bets across **12,208** accounts, **19** sports, 120-day window
- Generated by `data_generator/generate_sportsbook_data.py` with a fixed
  seed (42): anyone can regenerate the identical dataset and verify every
  finding in this portfolio against the generator's planted ground truth
- **No real player, wallet, or transaction appears anywhere in this
  repository or its history** — the dataset trades realism of
  individuals for realism of *patterns*, which buys something real data
  can never offer publicly: provable detector recall
- Planted patterns (sized from 15 years of live-book risk experience):
  ~460 scripted same-second accounts, 95 low-volume sharp profiles, a
  60-account insider-style group concentrated in one thin league, 20
  coordinated account pairs, and a recreational mass carrying a ~4% hold
- Per-bet fields: `bettor`, `bet_time`, `usd_amount`, `usd_ggr`,
  `sports`, `league`, `market`, `odds`, `resolved_odds`, `subbet_result`
- Account identifiers are sequential synthetic P-IDs (`P00001…P12208`)

## 2. The `resolved_odds` field-semantics trap

This dataset deliberately reproduces a field-semantics pitfall I hit in
my own trading-desk career: `resolved_odds` stores the **net return
multiplier** (decimal_odds − 1 on a win, 0.00 on a loss), not the
decimal odds. Every downstream consumer that assumes "odds" produces
silently wrong numbers — which is why the generator encodes it and the
test suite guards the corrected formula.

### Symptom

Initial KPI generation produced an `Avg_Odds` column with values below
1.0 (a representative row: `Avg_Odds = 0.71`). Decimal odds cannot be
below 1.0 by definition — the stake itself is always returned on a
winning bet, so the multiplier is bounded by 1.

### Investigation

Inspection of raw bet rows showed:

| `subbet_result` | `resolved_odds` value |
|---|---|
| `won` | `decimal_odds − 1` |
| `lost` | `0.00` |
| `cancelled` | varies |
| `cashout` | partial payout multiplier |

All 237,602 lost bets in the dataset carried `resolved_odds = 0`,
confirming the field is the **net return multiplier** (profit per unit
stake), not the decimal odds.

### Why the naive average broke

The original logic averaged `resolved_odds` across every bet a player
placed. Losing bets contributed zeros and dragged the average toward
zero, producing values like 0.71 that were mathematically impossible
for any odds metric.

### Worked example — player P3

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Total bets | 7 |
| Won bets | 4 |
| Sum of `resolved_odds` | 4.97 (only winning bets are non-zero) |
| Naive Avg_Odds | 4.97 / 7 = 0.71 |
| Correct Avg_Won_Odds | 4.97 / 4 + 1 = **2.24** |

A simple `+1` adjustment would have given 1.71, also wrong. The fix
requires both isolating winning bets and adding 1.

### Corrected formula

```
Avg_Won_Odds = mean(resolved_odds + 1)   over won bets only
```

Per-sport averages under the corrected method:

| Sport | Avg won odds |
|---|---|
| Football | 2.50 |
| Tennis | 2.67 |
| Basketball | 2.63 |
| Counter-Strike 2 | 3.02 |
| Table Tennis | 2.98 |

These align with realistic mid-market prices for each sport.

### Handling players who never won

Players with zero winning bets have no decimal-odds sample. These cells
are left blank rather than imputed to avoid biasing downstream
aggregations.

## 3. Bot detection — same-second placement

### Logic

A bot is operationally defined as an account that placed two or more
bets within the same one-second window. Implementation:

```python
df["sec"] = df["bet_datetime"].dt.floor("s")
counts = df.groupby(["bettor", "sec"]).size()
bot_suspects = counts[counts > 1].reset_index()["bettor"].nunique()
```

### Why this works

Manual placement requires reading the market, selecting the outcome,
entering a stake, and confirming. Even an experienced bettor on a
familiar platform rarely completes this in under two seconds. Two or
more placements in the same second on the same account is a
near-certain scripting signal.

### Known limitations

- A single false positive is possible from multi-bet slip submission
  (multiple selections confirmed in one click). Mitigation: cross-check
  by `subbet_count` per ticket before action.
- Distributed bots that rate-limit themselves to ≤1 placement/second
  will not be caught by this rule alone. A second-layer detector is
  needed for those (out of scope for this portfolio).

### Result

457 distinct accounts flagged. The flag is a *surveillance signal*,
not an automatic action — manual review is required before any account
restriction.

## 4. Multi-account similarity

### Approach

Each candidate account pair is scored on three dimensions, then
combined into a composite similarity score:

| Component | What it measures |
|---|---|
| **Sport similarity** | Cosine similarity of normalized sport-mix vectors |
| **Time similarity** | Overlap of betting-time histograms (hour-of-day) |
| **Stake similarity** | Distribution similarity of stake amounts |

The composite score is bounded 0-100. Pairs above 90 are flagged as
CRITICAL.

### Why these three

Operators running multiple accounts almost always inherit their own
preferences across accounts: the sports they understand, the hours they
trade, and the stake sizes their bankroll supports. Two of the three
matching is suggestive; all three matching at high similarity is
strongly indicative.

### Action gate

Similarity alone does not prove multi-accounting. Recommended workflow
before action:
1. Pull KYC records for both accounts
2. Compare device fingerprint, IP range, payment instrument
3. Escalate to compliance with the chain of evidence

## 5. Player risk scoring

### Feature set

| Feature | Rationale |
|---|---|
| Bet count | Volume signal |
| Total stake | Exposure signal |
| Net GGR | Direct house-side outcome |
| Average winning odds | Market preference / sharpness proxy |
| Win rate | Recent performance |
| CLV proxy | Sharpness (if available) |

### Output

A normalized score on a **0-1 scale** mapped to discrete bands:

| Score range | Band |
|---|---|
| ≥ 0.70 | CRITICAL |
| 0.50 - 0.69 | HIGH |
| 0.30 - 0.49 | MEDIUM |
| 0.15 - 0.29 | LOW |
| < 0.15 | MINIMAL |

### Known limitation

The model shows weak discrimination at the low-volume tail — a large
number of accounts cluster at exactly the baseline score (~0.2). This
is expected: with only a handful of bets, there is insufficient signal
to differentiate accounts. The dashboard's low-volume sharp-player flag
is the complementary check for this tail.

### Ensemble calibration notes

- **Log transform on volume features.** Z-scores in the ML ensemble are
  computed on `log1p` of stake and bet-count features. These
  distributions are heavily right-skewed; raw-scale z-scores over-flag
  the legitimate high-volume tail.
- **Isolation Forest contamination = 0.10.** This is a triage-capacity
  parameter, not a prevalence estimate: it caps the forest's flag share
  at roughly what the desk can review per cycle. It should be re-tuned
  whenever review capacity changes.
- **The three flags are correlated, not independent.** Z-score, IQR and
  Isolation Forest all consume the same five features. Agreement across
  them is therefore a *precision filter* that suppresses single-method
  noise — it is not three independent confirmations, and the score is
  never treated as a probability.

`fig.add_axes([x, y, w, h])` placement (0–1 figure-relative coordinates)
rather than the grid system to keep cards, headers, and tables on a
deterministic grid across runs.

### Why matplotlib instead of a templating tool

- Zero external dependencies beyond what the analytical pipeline
  already uses.
- Full control over every pixel — KPI cards, navy header band, table
  cell widths.
- Deterministic output for diffing reports week-over-week.

### Outputs

| Report | Cadence | Audience |
|---|---|---|
| Daily Risk Report | Daily | Risk desk |
| Weekly Executive Summary | Weekly | Management |

Both reports read from the same generated analysis files used by the
Streamlit dashboard, so data generation happens once upstream and every
output downstream is consistent.

## 7. Data provenance and generator design

### Why synthetic

Real AML cases, player behaviour, and book P&L cannot be published in
any form — even anonymized, aggregated operational metrics can be
proprietary. This portfolio resolves the conflict honestly: the data is
**fully synthetic**, the generator is committed, and provenance is
verifiable rather than asserted.

### What synthetic buys

Planted patterns mean **measurable recall**: the test suite asserts the
detector stack recovers the scripted accounts, the sharp profiles, and
the coordinated pairs on every CI run. A real dataset can never prove
its detector's recall publicly — ground truth is unknowable. This one
can.

### Generator design rules

- Single fixed seed; one command regenerates the full dataset bit-for-bit.
- Segment realism over individual realism: hold, odds distributions,
  circadian betting curves, and volume mixes match live-book shapes.
- Planted signals are *embedded in noise*, not isolated: lucky
  recreational accounts contaminate the sharp cohort, coincidental
  same-second placements contaminate the bot flag — so the detection
  problem stays honest.
- The one-day sample shipped in `Project_A_Dashboard/sample_data/` is a
  pure slice of the full dataset, regenerated by the same command.

## 8. Limitations and assumptions

- **Time zone.** All timestamps are treated as a single zone; if the
  source system writes in operator-local time, late-night placements
  may be misclassified as same-second activity around DST boundaries.
- **Cashouts.** Treated as a separate settlement state. Their
  `resolved_odds` values are not used in the average-odds computation.
- **Cancellations.** Excluded from all P&L and odds calculations.
- **Test accounts.** The generator plants none; a production deployment
  would flag internal QA accounts separately.

## 9. Multiple comparisons and the look-elsewhere effect

The UEFA Youth League finding (77.5% player win rate) was produced by a
scan across 19 sports and dozens of leagues. Any scan that wide will
surface extreme proportions somewhere by chance alone, so a raw "this
league looks anomalous" claim is exposed to the multiple-comparisons
objection.

Three controls keep the finding alive despite the scan:

1. **The Wilson lower bound, not the point estimate, carries the
   claim.** With 344 wins in 444 settled bets, the 95% Wilson interval
   is [73.4%, 81.1%]; the lower bound is ~18 percentage points above
   the 55% operational alert threshold. Under a Bonferroni-style
   correction across ~50 scanned league cells (effective z ≈ 3.29
   instead of 1.96), the interval widens to [70.3%, 83.3%] — the
   finding survives the harshest standard correction by 15 points.
2. **Effect size, not just significance.** A 77% win rate against
   markets priced near 2 average odds implies a player ROI far outside
   anything variance produces at this sample size; the dollar
   concentration (largest single block of unexplained house loss) makes
   it material independently of the p-value.
3. **Replication gate.** Per the senior review notes, no margin or
   settlement action is taken on a single observation window. The
   finding is routed to a tranche-based audit with a stop condition —
   exactly the posture appropriate for a result discovered by scanning.

The honest statement is: scanning found the candidate, the corrected
interval and the effect size justified escalation, and replication is
required before structural action. That is the standard the rest of
this portfolio holds itself to.

## 10. Same-second signal — false-positive classes

The ≥2-bets-in-one-second definition (§3) is deliberately a
**surveillance** definition, not an action definition, because at least
three legitimate behaviours can land multiple bets inside one second:

1. **Multi-leg / split slips.** A bet slip carrying several selections
   submitted as one batch writes several rows with one timestamp. These
   typically share the same market type or event cluster.
2. **Client-side retries.** Network retry logic can double-write a
   placement within the same second.
3. **Terminal / API partners.** B2B feeds and shop terminals
   legitimately batch placements.

Mitigations applied:

- The detector supports a stricter **layer-2 definition**
  (`distinct_markets_only=True` in `risk_core.detection`): the
  same-second bets must land on *different markets* to count, which
  clears the batch-slip class while keeping cross-market scripted
  activity flagged. This behaviour is unit-tested in
  `tests/test_detection.py`.
- Flagged accounts are routed to manual review with stake-density
  tiering (see the override log) — never to automatic action.
- Repeated breaches across multiple days, not a single same-second
  event, are the escalation criterion.
